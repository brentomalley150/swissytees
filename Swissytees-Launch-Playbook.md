*From "Launching Soon" to your first sale — the print-on-demand way.*
For Declan (and his business partner, Heinz) · June 2026

---

## How this works (the big idea)

We're using **print-on-demand (POD)**. That means: Declan uploads a design, a printing
company holds the blank shirts, and **only when someone buys a shirt does the partner
print it and mail it** to the customer. Declan never buys shirts ahead of time, never
stores boxes, and never ships anything himself.

**Why this is perfect for a 10-year-old founder:**

- **$0 upfront for shirts.** You only pay the print cost *after* a customer has already paid you.
- **No risk.** If a design doesn't sell, you've lost nothing.
- **No garage full of tees.** The partner does the printing, packing, and shipping.
- **You focus on the fun parts:** designs, the brand, and telling Heinz's story.

The trade-off: profit-per-shirt is smaller than buying in bulk. That's the right trade
when you're starting out — we trade a little profit for zero risk. Once a design proves
it sells, you *can* later order that one in bulk to make more per shirt.

## Part 1 — Store setup plan

### Step 1: Pick the print partner

The two big POD companies are **Printful** and **Printify**. (They announced a merger in
late 2025 but still run as separate services in 2026.) Here's the honest trade-off:

| | **Printful** | **Printify** |
|---|---|---|
| Base cost of a quality tee | ~$12–13 | ~$8–11 |
| Quality control | Owns its own print shops — very consistent | Uses many outside printers — quality varies |
| Ease for a beginner | Simplest, most polished | Slightly more setup (you pick a printer) |
| Monthly fee | Free to start ($29 Premium optional) | Free to start ($/Premium optional) |

**Recommendation: start with Printful.** Swissy Tees is positioned as the *premium, well-made*
option (not the cheap clip-art crowd), so consistent quality matters more than squeezing
every dollar at the start. You can always add Printify later for specific products to widen
margins. Order a **sample of your first shirt** so Declan can feel the quality and take real
photos — Printful gives members a sample discount.

**Recommended blank:** the **Bella+Canvas 3001** unisex tee — soft, true-to-size, the
industry standard for brands that care about feel. (Gildan 64000 is the cheaper option if
you want a lower price point later.)

### Step 2: Pick where customers check out

You own **swissytees.com**, and right now it shows the "coming soon" page on GitHub Pages.
To actually take orders, you connect a store platform. Two good paths:

**Path A — Shopify (recommended for a real brand).** Shopify is the standard home base for
apparel brands. You connect Printful as an app, and Shopify handles the cart, checkout, and
payments. When you're ready to sell, you point swissytees.com at Shopify (we'd just change
the same GoDaddy DNS settings we already used). Cost: Shopify often runs a **$1/month for the
first 3 months** intro, then ~$29–39/month. Best look, best control, your own domain.

**Path B — Etsy (cheapest, fastest, built-in shoppers).** Connect Printful to an Etsy shop.
No monthly fee — just ~$0.20 per listing plus Etsy's selling fees. Etsy *brings its own
traffic* of dog-and-gift shoppers, which is great for a first sale. Downside: less brand
control and the web address is etsy.com/shop/swissytees, not your domain.

**Suggested plan:** Launch on **Etsy first** to get early sales and reviews with zero monthly
cost and built-in traffic, *and* set up **Shopify** at swissytees.com as the real flagship
once a few designs prove themselves. Many small brands run both — Etsy for discovery, their
own site for brand and repeat customers.

### Step 3: Connect the dots

1. Create the Printful account → upload Declan's first designs onto the Bella+Canvas tee.
2. Create the store (Etsy and/or Shopify) and connect Printful as the fulfillment app.
3. Set prices (see Part 3) and publish 3–5 starter products.
4. Order **1 sample** to check quality and shoot real photos (Heinz can model near the box!).
5. Flip swissytees.com from "coming soon" to the live store when you're happy.
6. Keep the email sign-ups from the landing page — email everyone on launch day with a small
   discount code.

## Part 2 — First product designs

Start with **3–5 designs**, not 30. A tight, strong first drop is better than a cluttered shop.
Each concept below has the **shirt text**, the **art direction**, and which **audience** it hits.
Declan can sketch these or describe them to an AI image tool, then refine.

**1. The Hero Bernese Tee** *(widest audience — most buyers search "Bernese")*
A clean, friendly illustrated Bernese Mountain Dog (fluffy tricolor) — front and center,
art-forward, minimal text. Optional small "Swissy Tees" on the sleeve. This is the flagship.
*Why:* Bernese owners are the biggest slice of the market; lead with them for volume.

**2. "Owned by a Swissy"** *(funny owner-humor — very shareable on social)*
Bold text design: **"OWNED BY A SWISSY"** with a small paw print or dog silhouette.
*Why:* Owner-humor tees travel on Instagram/TikTok and make easy gifts.

**3. The Heinz Tribute / "Evergreen Mountain Dog Co." vibe** *(brand-story design)*
An alpine badge (just like your logo) featuring the dog on a mountain overlook with
**"Evergreen, Colorado"** in the ring — basically your logo as a wearable. Subtle nod to Heinz.
*Why:* Turns Declan and Heinz's real story into the product; locals and admirers love it.

**4. "Big Dogs. Bigger Hearts."** *(the tagline tee — sincere, premium)*
The brand tagline in a warm, clean typographic layout with a small Swiss-cross or paw accent.
*Why:* A design people wear even if they don't own the breed — broadens you to general buyers.

**5. The Rescue Tee** *(give-back collection)*
A heartfelt design where the product page says **"$X from every shirt supports Swiss Mountain
Dog rescue."** Pick one rescue to support and name it.
*Why:* The dog community rewards give-back brands with loyalty and word-of-mouth.

**Design rules to keep it on-brand:** stick to the badge palette (navy, rust/tan, white, Swiss
red), keep art clean and breed-accurate, and let the dog be the hero. Offer each design on
**cream, white, and heather navy** shirts to start.

## Part 3 — Pricing & money plan

### What it costs and what to charge

Using Printful's quality Bella+Canvas tee as the example:

| Item | Amount |
|---|---|
| What Printful charges you (base + printing) | ~$13 |
| **Your sell price** | **$28** |
| Payment processing fee (~3% + $0.30) | ~$1.15 |
| **Profit per shirt** | **~$13.85** |

A $28 price is right in the pocket for a premium dog tee — comparable brands (Teddy the Dog,
AKC Shop, indie dog-art shops) sell in the **$26–34** range, so you're competitive *and*
positioned as quality, not bargain-bin. Customers pay shipping at checkout, so it doesn't eat
your profit. Hoodies follow the same math at higher numbers (cost ~$30, sell ~$55–60).

### A simple kid-friendly way to think about money

For every shirt sold for **$28**, the money splits roughly like this:

- **$13** → pays the printer to make and mail the shirt
- **$1** → pays the card/checkout fee
- **$14** → **Declan's profit** 🎉

So **about half of every sale is profit** — and Declan didn't have to buy anything ahead of time.

### Startup costs (almost nothing)

- Shirts/inventory: **$0** (print-on-demand)
- Printful & Etsy accounts: **$0** to start
- One sample shirt to check quality: **~$15** (smart to do; one-time)
- Etsy listing fees: **~$0.20 per design**
- Optional Shopify later: **~$1/month** for the first 3 months

**Realistic money to start: about $20–30** for a sample and a few listings. That's it.

### A good first goal

Sell **10 shirts**. That's roughly **$140 in profit** — proof the brand works, real customer
photos, and first reviews. Then reinvest a little into one paid sample of the next design and
a small social push. Small, real, repeatable.
